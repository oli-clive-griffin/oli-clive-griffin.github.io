A time I got blocked on a simple problem

A problem that was too common for me as I was doing the kata was simple syntax errors. These were errors that should have been able to be fixed with a simple look at the documentation but instead, took ages to find.

I often used console logging to isolate the problem, for example, by logging the results of functions to see if they had worked as expected. However, once I had the problem isolated, which was fairly quick, I spent too long just blindly trying solutions without checking to see whether the most basic aspect of the code - the syntax -  was correct. Had I checked the documentation immediately after isolating the dysfunctional code, I might have saved an hour or so of blind and incredibly frustrating experimentation.

I will remember to make absolutely sure that my syntax is correct before trying other, more complex types of problem solving, because syntax errors are - at least for me - the most common, and most easily fixed problems i encounter while coding. 



A time I solved a problem in an elegant way

When solving the ‘where are you’ kata, I decided to use the array.find method. I wasn’t super familiar with the method syntax so after having a look at some examples in the documentation (I love examples) I copied and pasted an example into my code, replacing their variables with mine and paying attention to how this translated between the two contexts. 

This was a very fast and satisfying way of gaining a just-in-time understanding of the syntax. I think it’s important to realise that for most programming problems, especially the most fundamental ones like the basic syntax of a method, there’s almost always information readily available through google. Instead of trying to know everything, we should accept that we are practically cyborgs, with a large part of our “knowledge” stored online and ready at hand at a moment’s notice. 

I will continue to shamelessly consult google with my programming problems, embracing the concept of just-in-time learning. 



Reflect on how confident you feel using the problem solving techniques and process:

Pseudocode

I don’t do enough pseudocode. I tend to rush into problems without first creating a solid plan. That’s worked fine in the easier problems, but won’t work so well for more complex ones in the future. 

Trying something

I do this a lot, probably too much

Rubber ducky method

I explain things to myself often but I think it would make a difference if I did it more out loud, in a clear, logical, first principles based fashion, instead of my usual quiet muttering.  

Reading error messages

I pay close attention to error messages. They’re really helpful.

Console.logging

This is one of my main tools. I use console logging to get an look into how the code is running step by step. I will console log the results of functions or the values of variables etc. at different points throughout the code to make sure it’s working as planned. 

Googling

I do a lot of googling, it’s invaluable. 

Asking your peers for help

I try to rely more on myself and google but after a long enough struggle, i will sometimes ask my peers. I think it’s because I view this strategy as too slow, and perhaps it’s a bit of an ego thing.
